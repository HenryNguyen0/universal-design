Why

Diversity of Users

Universal Design and web accessibility directly impact the lives of over one billion people worldwide who live with disabilities, alongside others who face temporary or situational limitations. People are excluded from essential services, education, employment opportunities, and social connections due to inaccessible digital design. When a website isn't properly structured for screen readers, a blind person can't apply for a job. When videos lack captions, deaf users miss critical information. When interfaces require precise mouse movements, people with motor impairments can't access important services. This is about recognizing that all users, from elderly individuals to non-native language speakers, deserve equal access to digital resources.

Accessibility

Accessibility ensures that everyone, regardless of their physical, sensory, cognitive, or situational limitations, has equal access to digital content and services. By addressing diverse needs, we foster a more inclusive society where people with disabilities can fully participate in education, work, and social activities. In many countries, accessibility is a legal requirement under frameworks like the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in the U.S., the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG), and the European Accessibility Act. Non-compliance can lead to lawsuits, penalties, and reputational damage.

Accessibility drives innovation. Features like voice recognition, text-to-speech, and touch interfaces were initially designed for accessibility but are now widely used by everyone. Prioritizing accessibility can lead to new ideas that benefit a broader audience.

Benefits of Web Standards

The Web Standards Project (WaSP) emerged in 1998 to address inconsistencies in web development that created barriers for users, particularly those relying on assistive technologies. At the time, websites often catered to specific browsers, such as Internet Explorer or Netscape Navigator, resulting in fragmented user experiences. This lack of standardization disproportionately affected individuals with disabilities and others who depended on alternative means of accessing web content.

One of the main issues was how web developers used HTML. For example, they often relied on non-semantic tags, like /table/, to control the layout of a webpage. While visually effective, this method confused screen readers, which are designed to navigate through well-structured content. Users with visual impairments struggled because the underlying structure of these pages did not convey the intended relationships between elements.
